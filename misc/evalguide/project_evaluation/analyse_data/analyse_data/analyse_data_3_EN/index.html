Inferential statistics

Each different method involves distinct requirements as to the scale level of data (see Page Scale levels):


Testing of difference hypotheses

  • Methods for nominal data: If differences of frequency are to be analysed in the occurrence of certain attributes or attribute combinations (e.g. Are more female or more male students enrolled in social science courses? Has the number of non-smokers increased after an educational advertising campaign?), then the methods of χ2 are applied to data of nominal scale.
  • Methods for ordinal data (distribution-free methods): If two independent samples are to be compared regarding their central tendency (e.g. Do the students in one class have better scores than those in another class?), then the U-test of Mann-Whitney is used. If two dependent samples are to be compared (e.g. Do the students obtain better scores after training in comparison to the scores before training?), then the Wilcoxon-test is used.
  • Methods for interval and ratio data: In order to compare two independent sample means (e.g Do the students in a class need more time to solve a task than those in another class?), the t-test for independent samples is used; in a comparison of two dependent sample means (e.g. Do the students need to solve a task more time before the training than after the training?) the t-test for dependent samples is used.



Testing of correlational hypotheses

  • Methods for nominal data: As a measure to characterise the correlation of two attributes (e.g. Is the field of study chosen dependent on gender?) the contingency coefficient is employed to data of nominal scale.
  • Methods for ordinal data: The correlation of two ordinal-scaled attributes (e.g. Is the popularity of pupils associated with the school grade?) is captured with Spearman's ranking correlation coefficient.
  • Methods for interval and ratio data: The correlation between interval or ratio data (e.g. Is the time of solution of a task associated with the age?) is calculated using Pearson's product-moment correlation coefficient.
